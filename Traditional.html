Traditional clothing

All around the world traditional clothing has some significance , but in Lesotho traditional clothing is more than just a flashy fabric
- it tell the story of our history , our ancestors history,our values and
our way of life itself.In Lesotho each clothing has a meaning through its unique color and pattern

In this page you are going to learn about the traditional clothings of us basotho.

  • Seshoeshoe dress

    eucarlwears.com

    The Lesotho Seshoeshoe designs were originally introduced to the Basotho tribe by French missionaries during the 19th century. The fabrics were first patterned with designs representing the identity, wealth, and social status of the Lesotho people. It also serves as the Lesotho people’s traditional attire and fast became an integral part of the fashion scene in Lesotho.

  • Thethana

    smartshistory.org

    A thethana consists of a waistband upon which individually braided strands of fiber are attached. Historically, as a South Sotho girl reached the age of four or five she was typically dressed in a thethana, as it was often the only garment worn by young girls throughout childhood until the onset of puberty up until the early 20th century.

  • Mokorotlo

    The mokorotlo is a distinctive type of hat and is considered a national symbol of Lesotho, a small, landlocked country located within South Africa. It represents a mountain that is of great significance in Lesotho's history

  • Thepa

    The Thepa is a wraparound skirt or apron traditionally worn by Basotho women. It is usually made from thick woven fabrics or colorful cotton, emphasizing both functionality and aesthetic appeal . Its construction allows it to be worn over the seshoeshoe dress, the foundational Basotho female attire, or on its own in casual or domestic settings .

  • Lingoetsi

    The Lingoetsi blanket is specifically associated with brides in Basotho culture, having replaced the older Motlotlehi design for weddings .

  • beads

    Lesotho’s beadwork tradition is characterized by its vibrant colors, intricate designs, and the deep symbolic meaning embedded in each piece. Beads in Lesotho are not mere ornaments; they are a language in themselves, conveying messages about the wearer’s age, marital status, and even aspirations.

  • children clothes

    For everyday clothing, Basotho children often wear Basotho blankets (Seanamarena) made from thick wool to protect against the cold mountain weather

  • Balisana

    helplesotho.org

    Basotho herd boys typically wear woolen Basotho blankets, gumboots, and protective headgear such as woolen caps or balaclavas, combining cultural identity with practicality for mountain herding.

  • Poone

    Specific patterns like the "Poone" (corncob) motif symbolize fertility and wealth.

  • Tseha

types of lefitori(voctoria england)

The Lefitori blanket was initially designed and manufactured by the Wormald and Walker blanket mills in Yorkshire, England, to commemorate Queen Victoria’s diamond jubilee . In 1897, it was presented as a gift to King Letsie I, the successor of King Moshoeshoe I, marking a significant moment in Lesotho's history and reinforcing its ties with Britain.The blanket’s introduction coincided with a period of colonial and socio-political negotiation, during which Lesotho became a British protectorate largely for protection against encroaching settlers .

  • Badges of the brave

    This is red model of the "Badges of the Brave" design, part of the Victoria England collection, was created to honor Basotho soldiers who served and sacrificed in World War II. It incorporates motifs representing British Empire regimental badges from nations including the UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and South Africa, reflecting Lesotho’s close historical ties with the British during the war .

  • letlama blamket

    the Letlama is traditionally worn by individuals who have undergone initiation ceremonies, marking their transition into adulthood and maturity . The term "Letlama" connects to the phrase, "ke e tlametse mathata aseng basali ba batho" which translates to the wearer expressing they have tied the blanket for their own purposes, not for other men’s wives. This underscores the blanket’s role as a personal and symbolic garment.

  • Linare

    aranda.co.za

    The Linare Basotho Heritage Blanket was designed by Motsamai Moloko, a lecturer at Limkokwing University of Creative Technology, in celebration of King Letsie III’s birthday in 2009.The birthday celebration took place in the Leribe district; hence the design features the head of the buffalo which is an iconic representation of the district

    The Jubilee Ea Boipuso blanket was created to celebrate the 50th anniversary of an important national or royal event in Lesotho, marking it as a special ceremonial.
